Dataset Overview

VocalSet — Belt & Breathy Subset

~750 Total Samples
2 Classes
20 Singers
17 Features
9F / 11M Gender Split

VocalSet is a research dataset of professional singers performing musical scales, arpeggios, long tones, and song excerpts across 17 distinct vocal techniques. We subset the full dataset to the two techniques with the clearest acoustic contrast and most direct mapping to arousal level on Russell's Circumplex.

Files with silent passages, HNR values below −20 dB (Praat extraction artifacts), f0 outside the 50–500 Hz vocal range, or zero-valued spectral features were removed before analysis. The resulting dataset is approximately balanced between the two classes.

Feature Set

Extracted Acoustic Features

17 features per audio clip: 4 interpretable acoustic features and 13 MFCC coefficients capturing timbral texture beyond what single spectral statistics can express. MFCC coefficients approximate how humans perceive timbral texture, allowing the model to capture subtle vocal coloration beyond simple pitch and brightness statistics.

Feature Type Unit What it measures Expected difference (belt vs. breathy)
f0 Acoustic Hz Fundamental frequency (pitch) via YIN algorithm. Mean pitch across the clip. Belt higher — chest-dominant phonation raises fundamental frequency.
spectral_centroid Acoustic Hz Weighted mean frequency of the power spectrum. Measures timbral "brightness." Belt higher — louder, brighter harmonic series shifts centroid upward.
spectral_rolloff Acoustic Hz Frequency below which 85% of spectral energy lies. Measures high-frequency energy. Belt higher — correlated with centroid (r = 0.89); both track spectral brightness.
HNR Acoustic dB Harmonic-to-Noise Ratio via Praat. Measures periodicity vs. breathiness of the voice. Belt higher — clean glottal closure produces more periodic signal; breathy has more noise floor.
mfcc_0 – mfcc_12 MFCC — 13 Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients. Compact representation of the spectral envelope shape — capturing overall energy (mfcc_0) through increasingly fine timbral texture (mfcc_1–12). Strong class separation — MFCCs drove accuracy from 70% to 92.8% when added.
Feature Distributions

Acoustic Feature Histograms by Class

Per-feature distributions for belt and breathy classes plotted side-by-side. Overlap regions indicate where individual features are insufficient for discrimination; separation indicates discriminative power.

Figure 1 — Feature Distributions (Belt vs. Breathy)
Histogram bins colored by class. Features z-score normalized for display.

Interpretation

f0 and HNR show the clearest class separation — belt vocals cluster at higher fundamental frequencies and higher harmonic-to-noise ratios, consistent with the chest-dominant phonation mechanism.

Spectral centroid and rolloff distributions largely overlap between classes, confirming they are weaker individual discriminators. Their utility to the model is further limited by their near-redundancy (r = 0.89).

The MFCC coefficients — particularly mfcc_0 (log energy) and mfcc_1 — show strong bimodal distributions, with the two classes peaking in distinct regions. This is the primary driver of the accuracy jump from 70% to 92.8% when MFCCs are added.

Each histogram uses the same y-axis scale per feature to allow direct visual comparison of class density. Classes are approximately balanced (~52% belt, ~48% breathy).
Feature Correlation

Correlation Heatmap

Pearson correlation matrix across the four primary acoustic features. High off-diagonal values indicate redundancy; near-zero values indicate orthogonal, independent signal.

Figure 2 — Pearson Correlation Heatmap (17 features)
Values range from −1 (inverse) to +1 (perfect correlation). White = no correlation.

Interpretation

The strongest off-diagonal correlation is between spectral_centroid and spectral_rolloff (r = 0.89). Both features measure spectral brightness and carry nearly identical information — motivating the collinearity analysis in the PCA section of the Findings page.

Consecutive MFCC coefficients (e.g. mfcc_1 and mfcc_2) show moderate positive correlation, which is expected given the overlapping Mel filterbank basis functions. Despite this, each coefficient captures a distinct scale of timbral texture and the aggregate set provides strong discriminative power.

f0 and HNR are weakly correlated with the MFCC block, confirming they contribute independent acoustic information that complements the spectral envelope captured by MFCCs. Correlation values are reported using Pearson's r coefficient.

High intra-MFCC correlation is not a problem here — it reflects the physics of the Mel filterbank, not measurement redundancy. Regularized models like Logistic Regression handle this gracefully.
Key EDA Findings

Summary of Observations

Finding 01

Centroid & Rolloff Are Collinear

Pearson correlation of r = 0.89 between spectral centroid and spectral rolloff. Both features track the same underlying property — timbral brightness — and provide near-redundant information to linear classifiers.

Finding 02

HNR Artifacts Required Filtering

Praat's harmonicity algorithm returned values below −20 dB for some clips, indicating extraction failure rather than genuine signal. These rows were removed before modeling to avoid biasing the HNR feature distribution.

Finding 03

MFCCs Unlock Class Separation

The four base features alone topped out at 70% accuracy (Random Forest). Adding 13 MFCC coefficients pushed Logistic Regression to 91.4% (and XGBoost to 92.8%) — confirming that timbral texture beyond single spectral statistics is the dominant predictor.
