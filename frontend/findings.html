Results & Conclusions

Model accuracy comparison, confusion matrix analysis, feature importance, and key takeaways from the classification experiments.

Model Performance

Accuracy Comparison

Each classifier was evaluated on an 80/20 stratified train-test split. Two feature sets were tested: the base four acoustic features (f0, spectral centroid, spectral rolloff, HNR) and the expanded set that adds 13 MFCC coefficients capturing tone color and texture, bringing the total feature count to 17.

Model Base Features (4) + MFCCs (17) Δ Gain
Logistic Regression Deployed 58.6% 91.4% +32.8 pp
Random Forest ~70.0% 90.0% +20.0 pp
XGBoost — 92.8% highest overall
⚡
MFCCs were the decisive feature set. Adding 13 MFCC coefficients raised Logistic Regression accuracy from 58.6% to 91.4%, a gain of 32.8 percentage points. The base acoustic features (f0, spectral centroid, rolloff, HNR) were insufficient on their own. The overall tonal profile of the voice (the spectral envelope) captured by MFCCs contains the information that separates belt from breathy singing.

Dimensionality Reduction

PCA — 2D Feature Projection

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied to the four base acoustic features after z-score normalization to visualize how well the two vocal classes separate in two dimensions, and to identify which features carry overlapping information before modeling.

📊

pca_scatter.png

Figure 2. PCA Scatter (PC1 vs. PC2) — base acoustic features, z-score normalized

Interpretation

PC1 primarily captures the shared variance between spectral centroid and spectral rolloff (which correlate at r = 0.89), effectively combining these two overlapping brightness measurements into a single axis. PC2 separates along HNR and f0.

The partial overlap between belt and breathy clusters in 2D confirms that the four base features alone are not sufficient for clean separation, indicating the need for richer features like MFCCs.

The high correlation between spectral centroid and rolloff (surfaced by PCA) also explains why each contributes only ~4–6% of feature importance. They measure similar acoustic properties, so together they add limited unique information to the model.

Discrimination Ability

ROC Curves

ROC curves visualize how each classifier trades off between catching more true positives and introducing more false alarms, across every possible decision threshold. The area under the curve (AUC) summarizes this into a single score: 1.0 means perfect separation between classes, and 0.5 is no better than random guessing.

📈

Export from notebook → frontend/assets/

Figure 4. ROC curves — Logistic Regression vs. Random Forest (+ MFCC). Positive class = high_energy.

Interpretation

Both the Logistic Regression and Random Forest curves hug the upper-left corner, indicating high sensitivity with a low false alarm rate across thresholds. The AUC for both models exceeds 0.95, confirming that the classifiers generalize well beyond the default 0.5 decision threshold.

The close proximity of the two curves suggests diminishing returns from the more complex Random Forest. A simple linear classifier already captures most of the separation between the two vocal classes.

The diagonal dashed line represents a random classifier (AUC = 0.5). Both models are far above it, ruling out the possibility that results are inflated by class imbalance or overfitting.

Conclusions

Key Findings

Four central conclusions emerge from the classification experiments.

Finding 01

MFCCs are the decisive feature set

Base acoustic features (f0, centroid, rolloff, HNR) alone were not sufficient. Logistic Regression achieved only 58.6% without MFCCs, barely above chance on a two-class problem. Adding 13 MFCC coefficients raised accuracy to 91.4%, confirming that the differences between vocal techniques are captured in the overall tonal character of the voice (the spectral envelope), not just raw pitch or brightness.

Finding 02

A linear model is competitive with ensemble methods

XGBoost achieved the highest accuracy at 92.8%, with Logistic Regression close behind at 91.4% and Random Forest at 90.0%. The 1.4-point gap between the simplest and most complex model is narrow. Once MFCCs are included, the feature space becomes well-suited to linear classification, which explains why the simpler model performs nearly as well. Logistic Regression was selected for deployment because it offers comparable accuracy with far greater interpretability and a smaller serving footprint.

Finding 03

Spectral centroid and rolloff are redundant

PCA and EDA confirmed a Pearson correlation of r = 0.89 between spectral centroid and spectral rolloff. Both features measure the same acoustic property (timbral brightness, or how sharp and high-pitched the voice sounds), and together they contribute less than 10% of Random Forest feature importance. Removing one would have negligible impact on accuracy and would reduce redundancy in the feature set.

Finding 04

Vocal technique exhibits reliable acoustic patterns associated with arousal level

Reaching 92.8% accuracy on VocalSet's belt vs. breathy distinction supports the core thesis: singing technique carries consistent, measurable acoustic patterns tied to vocal energy level. Belt (powerful, high-effort delivery) and breathy (airy, low-energy tone) singing are reliably separable using MFCC features, supporting the use of technique-based acoustic signals in music recommendation pipelines.

Scope & Outlook

Limitations & Implications

A brief note on the scope of these results and what they suggest for downstream use.

Practical Implication

These results suggest that lightweight acoustic classifiers may be sufficient for real-time vocal affect tagging in music information retrieval (MIR) or recommendation systems, without requiring deep neural architectures.

Limitations
  • Single dataset. All results come from VocalSet — studio recordings of trained singers. Generalization to in-the-wild, live, or low-fidelity audio is untested.
  • Small test set. ~80 held-out clips after cleaning. Reported metrics carry meaningful variance — confidence intervals were not estimated.
  • No singer-level grouping. The 80/20 split is stratified on class but not on singer ID, so clips from the same singer can appear in both train and test. This may modestly inflate accuracy compared to a stricter setup where all clips from a given singer are kept entirely in one split.
  • Cultural and linguistic scope. VocalSet is predominantly Western-classical/pop technique; results may not transfer to other vocal traditions.
